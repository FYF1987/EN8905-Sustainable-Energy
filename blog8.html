Final Reflection & Conclusions:
An Honest Case for Bahrain’s Solar Future

Engineering judgement, personal growth, and a call to action

Posted: 13 July 2026  ·  Fahad Yousif Buradah

All Stages — Final Reflection

Summary of Findings Across All 5 Stages

StageKey Finding
Stage 1: SDG ResearchSDG 7 demands affordable, clean energy for all; 655 million people still lack electricity access worldwide. Bahrain’s solar resource (~2,150 kWh/m²/yr) and a doubled national target (20% renewables by 2035) make solar a real policy and engineering priority.
Stage 2: Energy AssessmentNatural gas (CCGT) scores well on reliability (~90% capacity factor, despatchable) but poorly on cost and emissions versus current solar figures. Solar PV scores best on global average cost ($0.043/kWh) and emissions (~41 g/kWh), but its 17.4% average capacity factor means it cannot yet replace gas outright.
Stage 3: Practical ActivityHome energy audit: 158.2 kWh/day total load, with air conditioning alone at 82% of it. Required solar array to offset this: 36 kWp (65 panels), avoiding an estimated 28.3 tCO₂/yr – calculated from first principles, with cost/payback figures deliberately left unverified rather than invented.
Stage 4: EthicsIEEE ethics support both the transition and honest disclosure of uncertainty. Agrivoltaics is a credible (though not yet locally confirmed) land-use mitigation. A fair transition needs real worker retraining and subsidy design that reaches low-income households, not just favourable headline numbers.
Stage 5: AwarenessA small-group session, 80% average quiz score. The “tree that grows electricity” analogy was most effective. Three colleagues expressed interest in rooftop solar – a small, real behavioural outcome.

Conclusion

Which system is more sustainable? Solar PV is the more sustainable system for Bahrain, on the evidence gathered here.

Why: Solar PV’s global average lifecycle emissions (~41 g CO₂/kWh) are roughly 12× lower than natural gas (~490 g/kWh), and its global average cost ($0.043/kWh, IRENA 2024) is already about 41% below the cheapest new fossil-fuel alternative – while gas remains exposed to volatile global fuel markets. Solar’s real weakness, a 17.4% average capacity factor versus gas’s ~90%, is a genuine engineering constraint, not a reason to dismiss it – it is an argument for pairing solar with storage and a phased, not overnight, transition.

How it supports SDG 7: Solar PV directly advances SDG 7.2 (raising the renewable share of the energy mix) and, through falling costs, SDG 7.1 (affordable, reliable access) – while continued near-total dependence on one imported fossil fuel does not.

Personal Reflection

This project transformed my understanding of energy systems from a set of isolated technical concepts into a connected, multidisciplinary field. I began with a basic view of solar panels as electrical devices, but through the research, calculations, corrections, and real-world activities in this project, I gained a far deeper and more integrated understanding of how engineering, economics, ethics, and policy all shape energy decisions.

I learned how to evaluate energy technologies using verified data rather than assumptions, and how to trace every figure back to authoritative sources like IRENA, the IEA, IPCC lifecycle-emissions literature, and peer‑reviewed irradiance studies. Developing this habit of evidence‑based engineering strengthened my technical judgement and taught me to treat every claim—especially my own—with healthy skepticism until it is verified.

Correcting earlier mistakes about the Al Dur project was one of the most valuable learning experiences. It taught me the importance of intellectual honesty, transparency, and the willingness to revise work when new information proves the old wrong. These are not just academic skills—they are core engineering competencies.

I also gained practical skills: conducting a full household energy audit, performing solar sizing calculations from first principles, interpreting irradiance data, and understanding how system losses, peak sun hours, and load profiles influence real-world design. These hands-on tasks made the theory tangible and showed me how engineering decisions directly affect everyday life.

Finally, the project strengthened my communication skills. Translating technical concepts into simple analogies—like the “tree that grows electricity”—showed me how powerful clear communication can be in shaping public understanding and encouraging sustainable choices.

Recommendations for Bahrain

  1. Verify and publish rooftop solar economics clearly: households need a trustworthy, EWA-confirmed figure for installation cost, tariff treatment, and payback – not estimates from unofficial sources.
  2. Continue Al Dur through to completion and monitor delivery: track the 100 MW project against its stated Q3 2027 completion target, and report progress publicly.
  3. Pair future solar expansion with storage: given a 17.4%-type capacity factor, battery storage sizing should be planned alongside any new solar capacity, not as an afterthought.
  4. Investigate an agrivoltaic pilot: worth feasibility-testing in Bahrain’s land-constrained context, following examples from elsewhere.
  5. Invest in workforce retraining: support gas-sector electrical and mechanical staff into solar installation and maintenance roles as the mix shifts.
Final Conclusion: On current global figures, solar PV is already cheaper and dramatically lower-emission than natural gas per kWh generated. Bahrain has a strong solar resource, a first utility-scale project under construction, and an official Energy Transition Plan targeting over 45% clean energy by 2040. The honest engineering position is not that the transition is finished or automatic – it is that the direction is well justified, the reliability challenge is real and solvable with storage, and the numbers should always be checked before they are repeated.
