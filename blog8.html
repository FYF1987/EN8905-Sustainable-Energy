Blog Entry 8 · Final Reflection & Conclusions

Final Reflection & Conclusions:
Solar PV is Bahrain's Energy Future

Engineering judgement, personal growth, and a call to action

Posted: 13 July 2026  ·  Fahad Yousif Buradah

All Stages — Final Reflection

Summary of Findings Across All 5 Stages

StageKey Finding
Stage 1: SDG ResearchSDG 7 demands affordable clean energy for all. Bahrain's 5.5 PSH/day and $0.022/kWh LCOE make solar PV the most powerful tool for meeting SDG 7.2's 40% renewables target. Al Dur Solar (1,500 MW) already proves the concept works at scale.
Stage 2: Energy AssessmentFossil fuel (CCGT) scores well on reliability (85–92% CF, despatchable) but poorly on sustainability (490–820 g CO₂/kWh). Solar PV scores best on cost ($0.022/kWh), emissions (45 g/kWh), and water use (0.03 L/kWh). Recommended: solar for daytime baseload + BESS + CCGT backup overnight.
Stage 3: Practical ActivityHome energy audit: 158.2 kWh/day total load. Required solar array: 36 kWp (65 panels). CO₂ avoided: 28.3 tCO₂/yr. Simple payback: 11.2 years (7.5 with subsidies). Calculation verified against PVSyst.
Stage 4: EthicsIEEE ethics mandate the transition. Agrivoltaics resolves land-use conflict. Progressive tariffs protect low-income households. Solar creates 5× more jobs per MW than gas. Bahrain's per-capita emissions create a moral obligation to decarbonise rapidly.
Stage 5: Awareness10 SABIC colleagues, 80% average quiz score. "Tree that grows money" analogy was most effective. 3 colleagues interested in personal rooftop solar — real-world awareness converting to action.

Personal Reflection

Before this project, I understood solar panels as electrical components. I now understand them as a systemic solution to climate justice, energy poverty, economic stability, and national security simultaneously.

The home energy audit was the most grounding exercise — seeing that my family's AC alone consumes 129.6 kWh/day made the abstract concept of energy transition personal. A 36 kWp rooftop system could eliminate our household's entire fossil fuel electricity footprint.

The social awareness session reinforced something important: technical correctness is necessary but not sufficient. The "tree that grows money" analogy converted a dry engineering comparison into something emotionally compelling — and three colleagues immediately wanted to know how to install solar on their own homes.

Recommendations for Bahrain

  1. Accelerate rooftop solar programme: Subsidised installation for residential users, especially low-income — targeting 500 MW by 2028
  2. Mandate solar on all new construction: Building regulations requiring solar-ready roofs and pre-installed panels on new developments
  3. Deploy grid-scale BESS: 500 MWh battery storage to complement Al Dur and future solar plants for evening peak management
  4. Launch agrivoltaic pilot: 50 MW agrivoltaic farm combining solar generation with date palm or vegetable cultivation
  5. Establish solar skills centre: SABIC and EWA should co-fund a solar O&M training facility — creating Bahraini jobs for the transition
Final Conclusion: Solar PV is already the cheapest electricity source in Bahrain ($0.022/kWh), produces 97% less CO₂ than gas, uses 98% less water, and creates more jobs per MW than any fossil fuel alternative. Transitioning Bahrain's grid to 80% solar by 2040 is technically feasible, economically superior, ethically imperative, and directly aligned with SDG 7. As an electrical engineer at SABIC, I will champion this from within industry.
