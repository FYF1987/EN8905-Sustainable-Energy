Bahrain Solar Context:
A Strong Resource, Just Getting Started

Why Bahrain’s geography favours solar – and an honest look at where the Al Dur project actually stands

Posted: 23 June 2026  ·  Fahad Yousif Buradah

Stage 1 — Regional Energy Need
Correction from the original draft: An earlier version of this blog stated that the Al Dur Solar plant was a 1,500 MW facility, operational since 2024. Checking official and industry-press sources shows this was wrong on both counts. The corrected facts are below.

Bahrain’s Solar Resource

Bahrain sits in a strong solar-irradiation zone. Peer-reviewed measurements put Bahrain’s Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI) at approximately 2,125–2,160 kWh/m²/year, with average daily sunshine exceeding 10 hours. That is a large, steady resource relative to the country’s small land area and electricity demand.

Al Dur Solar – the Correct, Current Status

The Al Dur Solar PV project, developed by Bahrain’s Electricity and Water Authority (EWA), is a 100 MW ground-mounted plant – not 1,500 MW. Its foundation stone was laid in late 2025/early 2026, and as of the most recent industry reporting it is still under construction, with completion expected around Q3 2027 (some project trackers place commercial operation as late as 2028).

Al Dur Key Facts (verified): 100 MW capacity · ~135,000 solar panels · ~830,000 m² across two sites in the Al Dur area, Southern Governorate · connects to the grid via the 66/11 kV Al Dur BSP substation · developed and owned by EWA · part of the push toward Bahrain’s 20%-by-2035 renewables target.

Bahrain’s Current Generation Mix – the Honest Picture

It is important to be direct about where Bahrain starts from: IEA data reported by the World Bank shows Bahrain’s electricity generation was essentially 100% natural gas through the early 2020s (99.7–99.9% in 2021–2022), and industry tracking for 2023 still put natural gas at roughly 73–74% of electricity generated once newer solar capacity is counted in. In short: Bahrain today is one of the most gas-dependent grids in the world, and Al Dur (100 MW) is a first, still-under-construction step – not a completed transition.

Where the Transition Is Actually Headed

EWA’s own Energy Transition Plan (completed November 2023) sets out a more ambitious pathway than the NREP alone: cutting natural gas use by more than 30%, and meeting over 45% of power demand from clean sources by 2040, alongside a 30% cut in emissions by 2035. This is the credible, official basis for judging Bahrain’s solar trajectory – not headline capacity figures for a single project.

Ground-mounted solar array, illustrative (not the Al Dur site – no public site photography was available at time of writing)
Conclusion for Blog 2: Bahrain has a genuinely strong solar resource and a real, funded first utility-scale project – but it is still under construction, sized at 100 MW, and starting from a grid that is almost entirely gas-fired today. The case for accelerating solar is strong; the starting point is further back than the original draft suggested.
