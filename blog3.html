Natural-Gas Power Generation in Bahrain

Technical, economic and environmental analysis of Bahrain’s gas-fired grid

Posted: 27 June 2026  ·  Fahad Yousif Buradah

Stage 2 — Current Energy System Assessment

Bahrain’s Gas-Fired Grid

Bahrain’s electricity system is, today, almost entirely natural-gas fired. World Bank/IEA data show natural gas supplied 99.7–99.9% of generation in 2021–2022, and even with early solar capacity coming online, natural gas still accounted for roughly 73–74% of electricity generated in 2023 per Climatescope tracking (the gap between the two figures reflects how capacity share and generation share differ, and how quickly the picture can move as new plants like Al Dur 2 and early solar come online). Combined-cycle gas turbine (CCGT) plants are the workhorse technology, supplemented by older, less efficient units.

Technical Strengths of Gas-Fired Power

  • Despatchability: output can ramp up within minutes to meet peak summer air-conditioning demand
  • High capacity factor: can generate continuously, independent of weather or time of day
  • Grid inertia: synchronous generators help stabilise grid frequency
  • Proven, sunk infrastructure: existing plants, pipelines and grid connections are already built and paid for

Stage 2 Assessment – Natural Gas: Technically strong for reliability and grid stability, and still the backbone of Bahrain’s system today. But a grid this concentrated in one fossil fuel sits in direct tension with SDG 7.2 (renewable share) and SDG 13 (climate). EWA’s own Energy Transition Plan acknowledges this and targets a real, phased reduction in gas dependence – not an overnight switch.
