Ethical Responsibilities & Social Dimensions
of the Solar Energy Transition

Who benefits, who bears the cost, and what engineering ethics demands

Posted: 6 July 2026  ·  Fahad Yousif Buradah

Stage 4 — Ethics & Social Dimensions

Ethical Framework: IEEE Code of Ethics

As an electrical engineering student, I reference the IEEE Code of Ethics – holding paramount the safety, health and welfare of the public, disclosing factors that might endanger it, and being honest and realistic in technical claims. That last principle is exactly why Blog 2 and Blog 5 openly correct and flag figures I could not verify, rather than presenting convenient numbers as fact.

Ethical Trade-offs in the Solar Transition

Ethical IssueNatural Gas (Status Quo)Solar PV (Transition)
Climate responsibilityContinues significant per-kWh emissions from a grid that is almost entirely gas-firedDirectly reduces Bahrain’s per-capita power-sector emissions
Energy affordabilityCurrently subsidised; end-user cost is low but tied to global gas marketsGlobal solar LCOE already below the cheapest new fossil-fuel alternative (IRENA, 2024)
Land useCompact plants relative to outputUtility-scale solar needs significant land area; agrivoltaics (combining solar with agriculture) is one proposed mitigation
Reliability equityDespatchable 24/7 – keeps critical loads (hospitals, cooling) running at nightDaylight-only output means solar alone cannot yet guarantee equal reliability without storage/backup
Worker transitionExisting gas-sector jobs and skills are concentrated here todayElectrical and mechanical skills used in gas plants transfer directly to solar installation and O&M roles
Global energy povertyCentralised fossil generation does little for the 655 million people still without electricity access worldwideOff-grid and mini-grid solar is a direct, low-cost tool for SDG 7.1 (universal access)

Who Is Affected?

Locally (Bahrain)

  • Households: air conditioning is the single largest residential electricity load (my own audit in Blog 5 found it at 82% of household consumption) – so efficiency and solar rooftop measures matter directly to bills
  • Low-income households: need well-designed subsidy or financing schemes to benefit from rooftop solar, not just households that can pay upfront
  • Workers in the gas sector: a fair transition means genuine retraining pathways into solar O&M, not just displacement

Globally

  • Climate-vulnerable countries: every tonne of CO₂ avoided in high-income, high-consumption countries like Bahrain reduces cumulative global warming that falls hardest on low-emitting, vulnerable nations
  • The 655 million without electricity access: the same falling solar costs that help Bahrain also make off-grid electrification newly affordable elsewhere
Agrivoltaic installation combining solar panels with agriculture
Agrivoltaics – a real land-use mitigation: Combining solar panels with agriculture (agrivoltaics) is an active area of research and deployment worldwide, and is one credible way to reduce the land-use trade-off of utility-scale solar in a small, dense country like Bahrain – though I have not found a confirmed agrivoltaic project operating in Bahrain specifically, so this remains a recommendation rather than a documented local example.
Ethical Conclusion: Under IEEE ethics, honest disclosure of technical uncertainty is itself an ethical duty. On that basis, the solar transition is well justified on climate and equity grounds, but a genuinely ethical transition plan needs: progressive tariff/financing design so low-income households actually benefit, real worker retraining programmes, and transparent, verifiable public communication of costs and benefits – not just favourable headline figures.
