of the Solar Energy Transition

Who benefits, who bears the cost, and what engineering ethics demands

Stage 4 — Ethics & Social Dimensions

Ethical Framework: IEEE Code of Ethics

As an electrical engineer, I reference the IEEE Code of Ethics — holding paramount the safety, health, and welfare of the public, contributing to sustainable development, and being honest in technical assessments. I also draw on the UN Engineers Without Borders ethical framework emphasising equity and do-no-harm.

Ethical Trade-offs in the Solar Transition

Ethical IssueFossil Fuel (Status Quo)Solar PV (Transition)
Climate justiceContinues emissions harming vulnerable populations globallyReduces Bahrain's per-capita emissions — one of world's highest
Energy affordabilityCurrently subsidised — low end-user costSolar LCOE $0.022/kWh — already cheapest electricity source
Land useCompact power plantsLarge land area needed — agrivoltaics (solar + farming) as solution
Water use1.8 L/kWh cooling — critical in water-scarce Gulf~0.03 L/kWh panel cleaning — 98% water saving
Worker transitionExisting fossil fuel jobs at riskSolar creates 5× more jobs per MW than gas (IRENA)
Energy poverty (global)Fossil fuel inaccessible for 733M off-grid poorOff-grid solar directly addresses SDG 7.1 access goal

Who Is Affected?

Locally (Bahrain)

  • Households: Solar reduces electricity bills — AC accounts for ~70% of Bahrain residential bills in summer
  • Industrial users (SABIC): Lower LCOE solar reduces operating costs for energy-intensive industries
  • Low-income households: Require subsidised rooftop solar installation to benefit — equity design essential
  • Fossil fuel workers: Retraining programme needed — electrical and mechanical skills transfer directly to solar O&M

Globally

  • Climate-vulnerable nations: Every tonne of CO₂ Bahrain avoids reduces global warming harm to Pacific islands, Bangladesh, and sub-Saharan Africa
  • Energy-poor communities: Off-grid solar is the only affordable electrification solution for the final 733 million
Agrivoltaics — the Land-Use Solution: Combining solar panels with agriculture (agrivoltaics) solves the land-use ethical dilemma. Shade from panels reduces crop water stress by 30% in hot climates — highly relevant for Bahrain's food security.
Ethical Conclusion: The solar transition is ethically imperative under IEEE ethics. Key requirements: progressive tariff design protecting low-income users, worker retraining programmes, agrivoltaic deployment for food security, and transparent public communication of benefits.
